SLIDE 6 — THE 8 META-RULES (1 & 2)
(~2.1 min)
================================================================================

Alright, meta-rule number one, and this is the one the exam tests the most.

When a requirement is deterministic — when something must always happen — you
enforce it programmatically, with a hook or a prerequisite gate. You do not rely
on a prompt instruction. Think about it: if your agent must verify a customer's
identity before it processes a refund, or it must never issue a refund above a
certain limit — those are guarantees. And a prompt, no matter how strongly you
word it, has a non-zero failure rate. It'll comply most of the time, but "most of
the time" is not acceptable when money or safety is on the line. So the moment you
see the words "must," "always," or a financial or identity requirement — your
answer is a hook or a gate, never a prompt.

Meta-rule number two: prefer the proportionate, root-cause fix. When a question
asks for the "first step" or the "most effective" change, it's usually looking for
the lowest-effort fix that actually addresses the real cause of the problem. And
this is where people get tricked — because there's almost always a tempting,
impressive-sounding option: train a classifier, add a machine-learning routing
layer, deploy a second model. Cross those out. If a simple prompt fix or a better
tool description hasn't even been tried yet, the fancy solution is over-engineered,
and it's the wrong answer. Match the size of the fix to the size of the problem.


================================================================================
SLIDE 7 — THE 8 META-RULES (3 & 4)
(~2.1 min)
================================================================================

Meta-rule number three is about how the model actually picks a tool. Tool
descriptions are the primary mechanism the model uses to choose between tools. So
when you see a scenario where the agent keeps calling the wrong tool — it grabs
"get customer" when the user asked about an order, and both tools have these thin,
one-line descriptions — the fix is to rewrite the descriptions. Make them explain
the inputs they take, give example queries, describe edge cases, and say when to
use this tool versus a similar one. That beats adding few-shot examples, it beats
building a routing layer, and it beats merging the tools together. Descriptions
first. That's almost always the correct first step for tool confusion.

Meta-rule number four is a quiet one, but it eliminates a lot of distractors.
Self-reported confidence and sentiment are unreliable proxies. So any answer that
says "have the model rate its own confidence and escalate when it's low" — cross
it out. Why? Because a language model's confidence is poorly calibrated. On the
hard cases, the ones you actually want to catch, it's often confidently wrong. And
the same goes for sentiment analysis. A frustrated customer is not the same as a
complex case — sentiment doesn't track difficulty. So whenever an option routes,
escalates, or makes a decision based on the model's own confidence score or on
customer emotion, that's your signal it's a trap.


================================================================================
SLIDE 8 — THE 8 META-RULES (5 & 6)
(~2.1 min)
================================================================================

Meta-rule number five: least privilege for tools. The more tools you give an
agent, the worse it gets at choosing the right one. There's a classic example on
this exam — an agent with something like eighteen tools that keeps misusing them,
versus scoping it down to the four or five it actually needs for its role. Fewer,
well-chosen tools means more reliable selection. And when an agent occasionally
needs something outside its role — say a synthesis agent that needs to do a quick
fact check now and then — you don't hand it every search tool in the system. You
give it one narrow, scoped tool for that specific need, and you route the
complicated cases back through the coordinator. Scope tightly.

Meta-rule number six is one I see people miss constantly. A bigger context window,
or a bigger model, does not fix attention. Here's the setup: you're reviewing a
pull request with a lot of files in one pass, and the review comes back
inconsistent — deep on some files, shallow on others, even contradicting itself.
That's attention dilution. And the tempting answer is "use a model with a larger
context window so it can see everything at once." Wrong. A bigger context doesn't
improve the quality of attention across all that content. The real fix is to split
the work — do a focused pass on each file for local issues, then a separate
integration pass for the cross-file stuff. Split into passes, don't scale up.


================================================================================
SLIDE 9 — THE 8 META-RULES (7 & 8)
(~2.1 min)
================================================================================

Meta-rule number seven: structured data beats prose, basically everywhere on this
exam. Whenever you're choosing between passing information as free-flowing text
versus passing it as structured data, the structured option is almost always
right. When a tool fails, return structured error context — the failure type,
what was attempted, whether it's retryable — not a generic "operation failed."
When you're combining research from multiple sources, preserve structured
claim-and-source mappings, so attribution survives the synthesis step. And in a
long support conversation, keep the key facts — the amounts, the order numbers,
the dates — in a persistent, structured "case facts" block, instead of letting
them get flattened into a vague summary. If an option keeps the structure and the
metadata, favor it.

And meta-rule number eight: diagnose the actual failing component. This one's about
reading the evidence carefully. The exam loves to give you a scenario where several
agents are involved, and then drop a clue in the logs. For example, the final
report is missing whole topics, and the logs show the coordinator decomposed the
task too narrowly. In that case, the fault is the coordinator — not the subagents,
which correctly executed the flawed plan they were handed. So don't blame a
component that actually did its job. Read the log detail in the question — it
usually points straight at the culprit — and match your fix to that.

SLIDE 10 — AUTO-ELIMINATE: FEATURES THAT DON'T EXIST
(~1.5 min)
================================================================================

Okay, let's talk about free points. Because some questions include answer options
that reference features which simply do not exist. If you can recognize them, you
eliminate them instantly.

Here are the three big ones. First, there is no environment variable called
CLAUDE_HEADLESS. If you want to run Claude Code non-interactively — say, in a CI
pipeline so it doesn't hang waiting for input — the real flag is dash-p, or
dash-dash-print. That's it.

Second, there is no dash-dash-batch flag on the Claude Code command line. Batch
processing is a completely separate thing — it's the Message Batches API, which
you call for large, non-urgent workloads. It is not a CLI flag you tack onto a
command.

And third, there's no "commands" array inside a dot-claude slash config-dot-json
file. Custom slash commands live as files in the dot-claude slash commands
directory. That's how they get shared with your team through version control.

So memorize the real answers on the right side of this slide. When a distractor
mentions one of these three fake features, it's gone. Cross it out and move on.


================================================================================
SLIDE 11 — CONFUSABLE PAIRS WHERE POINTS HIDE
(~2.0 min)
================================================================================

Now, this slide is where a lot of near-miss scores are actually lost. These are
pairs of concepts that are easy to mix up, and the exam deliberately puts both of
them in the answer options. Let me walk you through them quickly, and your homework
is to drill these until the distinction is automatic.

Resume versus fork versus fresh start. You resume a session to continue where you
left off when the context is still valid. You fork a session to branch off and
explore a different approach from the same starting point. And you start fresh with
an injected summary when your old results have gone stale — for example, after the
code has changed a lot.

Resource versus tool in MCP. A resource is read-only content — a catalog, a schema,
a list. A tool is an action the agent performs.

Tool choice — "any" versus forced. "Any" means the model must call some tool, but
it chooses which. Forced means it must call one specific tool you named.

PostToolUse versus a call-interception hook. PostToolUse transforms results after a
tool runs. Interception blocks or redirects an outgoing call before it happens.

Grep versus Glob. Grep searches inside file contents. Glob matches file names and
paths.

And plan mode versus direct execution. Plan mode for architectural, multi-file
work with several possible approaches. Direct execution for a simple, well-scoped
change. Learn these six pairs cold.

SLIDE 12 — ANSWERS THAT ARE ALMOST ALWAYS WRONG
(~1.7 min)
================================================================================

Let's flip it around. Instead of hunting for the right answer, sometimes it's
faster to recognize the wrong ones. Here are the distractor patterns that are
almost always incorrect on this exam.

Training a classifier or adding a machine-learning routing layer, when a simple
prompt or description fix hasn't been tried. Using the model's own self-reported
confidence to route the hard cases. Using sentiment as a stand-in for how complex a
case is. Reaching for a bigger model or a larger context window to fix what's
really an attention problem. Consensus voting that only flags an issue if it shows
up in multiple runs — which sounds careful, but actually suppresses real bugs that
only get caught intermittently. Using a prompt instruction where the situation
clearly demands a deterministic hook or gate. Silently swallowing errors, or
killing an entire workflow because one small piece failed. And of course,
referencing those non-existent features we just covered.

If an answer option matches any of these patterns, it's very likely the trap. Train
yourself to spot them on sight. This kind of fast, confident elimination is exactly
what buys you time and accuracy under pressure.

SLIDE 14 — A FOCUSED STUDY PLAN
(~2.1 min)
================================================================================

Here's the thing about this exam — it rewards people who have actually built with
these tools, because it's testing your judgment about tradeoffs. So the best
preparation is to build small versions of each system. Let me give you six
workstreams.

One: build an agent. Implement a full agentic loop — driven by the stop reason,
with tool calls, structured error handling, and session management. Practice
spawning subagents and passing context between them, because that's the biggest
domain.

Two: configure Claude Code for a real project. Set up the CLAUDE.md hierarchy,
create path-scoped rules with glob patterns, build a skill with frontmatter options
like context-fork, and wire in at least one MCP server.

Three: design MCP tools. Write descriptions that clearly differentiate similar
tools, and implement structured error responses with error categories and
retryable flags.

Four: build a structured data extraction pipeline. Use tool-use with a JSON schema,
make fields nullable so the model doesn't fabricate, add a validation-and-retry
loop, and try batch processing with custom IDs.

Five: practice the prompt techniques — few-shot examples for ambiguous cases,
explicit criteria to cut false positives, and multi-pass reviews.

And six: study context management and escalation — case-facts blocks, scratchpad
files, subagent delegation, and knowing when to escalate versus resolve. Every one
of these maps directly to an exam scenario.


================================================================================
SLIDE 15 — PRACTICE THE WAY THE EXAM REWARDS
(~1.8 min)
================================================================================

Building gives you the knowledge. But how you practice questions is what turns that
knowledge into a passing score. So let me give you four habits.

First, work in timed scenario blocks. Do about ten questions in twenty minutes.
This builds the two-minute-per-item rhythm you'll need on the real thing, so the
clock never surprises you.

Second — and this is the single highest-value habit — name the trap out loud. For
every wrong option, say why it's wrong. "That one uses a prompt where it needs a
gate." "That one relies on self-confidence." This is the exact skill that converts
a near-miss into a pass, because it trains fast elimination.

Third, use active recall, not re-reading. Re-reading your notes feels productive,
but it's weak. Instead, cover the answer and try to recall it. Cover the
confusable-pairs list and reproduce the definitions from memory. Retrieval is what
makes it stick.

And fourth, redo what you miss. Come back to the questions you got wrong a day
later and re-attempt them. Even better, track which meta-rule each miss maps to —
if you keep missing the deterministic-versus-prompt questions, you know exactly
what to review.

SLIDE 16 — EXAM-DAY STRATEGY & TIME MANAGEMENT
(~2.0 min)
================================================================================

Okay, it's exam day. Let's talk about how to actually take the test, because good
process here is worth real points.

Step one: read the whole question stem before you look at the options. The deciding
detail is very often buried in a sentence like "the logs show..." — and that
detail frequently names the culprit for you. Don't skim past it.

Step two: classify the requirement. Ask yourself — is this a deterministic
requirement, something that must always happen? Or is it best-effort? That single
call instantly narrows your answer toward a hook or gate versus a prompt.

Step three: eliminate before you choose. Cross out the fake features, the
over-engineered solutions, and anything built on sentiment or self-confidence.
Often you're down to two options before you even think hard.

Step four: mind the clock. About two minutes per item. Answer the reflex questions
fast — the scope questions, the CLI flags, the stop-reason ones — and bank that
time for the close judgment calls that actually need it. Don't let two hard
questions eat the time meant for ten easy ones.

And step five: on any "select two" item, pick exactly the number requested. Not
one, not three. Read the instruction and match it.
